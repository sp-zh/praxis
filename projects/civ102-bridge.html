Bridge Project

Team 304 designed and built a matboard box girder bridge, iterating from spreadsheet/MATLAB analysis to physical prototyping. The final concept is a truss-like box girder: a rectangular box reinforced with triangular internal diaphragms and targeted deck stiffening aligned with bending demand.

1) Requirements & scoring context

Frame the problem properly before designing anything.

  • Primary objectives: design/build across a 1200 mm span (assumed), and predict failure using beam theory + thin plate buckling theory.
  • Hard constraints: length 1250–1270 mm; deck ≥ 100 mm wide and unobstructed; deck height < 200 mm above supports and multiple of 20 mm; 60 mm flat support regions at both ends.
  • Performance scoring factors: test failure load, strength-to-weight ratio, accuracy of predicted failure load, and construction quality.
Framing choice: treat “governing failure mode” as the driver. We converged by eliminating designs dominated by buckling / shear transfer / joints rather than optimizing only bending yield.

2) Final design snapshot

What we built, with rationale attached to each feature.

  • Overall concept: box girder with a single offset splice placed ~1/3 along the length to reduce risk near maximum-moment region.
  • Cross-section: 80 mm wide × 60 mm tall structural box; 100 mm wide deck; total bridge length 1250 mm.
  • Internal structure: zigzag triangular infill forming 23 diaphragms at ~60°.
  • Deck reinforcement: 3 extra layers under the deck across three centered regions (205–1045, 300–950, 385–865 mm) to match bending moment demand.

3) Analysis workflow

Represent the engineering reasoning, not just the geometry.

  • Step A: identify critical train positions and build SFD/BMD envelopes.
  • Step B: compute stresses (σ = My/I, shear flow q = VQ/I) and check multiple failure modes.
  • Step C: compute factor of safety (FOS) for each mode; the minimum governs and drives iteration.

4) Major iterations

Each iteration is a claim → justification → evidence → decision.

  • HSS baseline: revealed centroid/compression risk and buckling vulnerability without diaphragms.
  • Height/width sweeps: improved yield margins but showed buckling-dominant regimes.
  • I-girder concept: strong for yield but high risk in local buckling, shear buckling, and glue-joint shear.
  • Triangular diaphragm prototypes: orientation tests informed final zigzag internal structure.
  • Final convergence: truss-box approach + targeted deck layers for moment-matched reinforcement.

8) Reflection

What this project changed in my design practice.

  • Key shift: governing failure mode thinking beats single-metric optimization.
  • Practice upgrade: requirements → sweeps → prototype risky assumptions → converge via governing mode.
  • Next iteration: quantify glue-joint shear earlier; tighten evidence trail for each major design claim.
