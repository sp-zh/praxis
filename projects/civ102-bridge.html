Bridge Project

Team 304 designed and built a matboard box girder bridge and iterated from spreadsheet/MATLAB analysis to physical prototypes. We converged on a “truss box girder” concept: a rectangular box girder reinforced with triangulated internal diaphragms and targeted deck stiffening aligned with the bending moment demand.

1) Requirements & scoring context

Frame the problem properly before designing anything.

  • Assignment structure: The CIV102 bridge project includes structural calculations, writing components, and physical build/performance.
  • Primary objectives: design and build across a 1200 mm span (assumed), and predict failure using beam theory + thin plate buckling theory.
  • Hard constraints (examples): total length must be within 1250–1270 mm; deck must be ≥ 100 mm wide, horizontal, and allow unobstructed train passage; deck height must be < 200 mm above supports and be a multiple of 20 mm; bridge must have 60 mm flat bottom portions at both ends for supports.
  • Performance scoring factors: test failure load, strength-to-weight ratio, and accuracy of predicted failure weight; construction quality also matters.
My framing choice: treat “governing failure mode” as the core design driver. I optimized for a design that is hard to fail via compression-side yielding or local buckling under realistic load paths, rather than optimizing one metric in isolation.

2) Final design snapshot

What we built, with the “why” attached to each feature.

  • Overall concept: A box girder with a single offset splice placed ~1/3 of the span to reduce risk near the maximum moment region.
  • Cross-section: 80 mm wide × 60 mm tall structural box; 100 mm wide deck; total bridge length 1250 mm; both ends capped with diaphragms.
  • Internal structure: a continuous zigzag triangular infill built from 5 matboard pieces forms 23 evenly spaced diaphragms at 60°.
  • Deck reinforcement (moment-matched): 3 extra layers adhered under the deck over three centered regions: 205–1045 mm, 300–950 mm, and 385–865 mm (full-width).
  • Glue strategy: full-width glue tabs to increase bonding area, simplify geometry, and improve constructibility (folds to flat surfaces).
  • Why 60 mm height: height is a tradeoff: higher improves compressive-yield FOS but reduces web local buckling FOS; analysis indicated staying below 80 mm is necessary for the 1000 N target without consuming excessive material.
  • Why 80 mm structural width with a wider deck: reducing width increases yield FOS while web buckling stays similar; the deck can overhang because train width is 75 mm (10 mm margin per side).
One-line design intent: “Move material into flanges + shorten/slenderness-control the compression panels + keep shear paths stable.”

3) Analysis workflow (how we predicted failure)

Represent the engineering reasoning, not just the final geometry.

  • Step A — Identify critical train positions: find train locations that maximize shear and bending moment for the specified load case(s).
  • Step B — Build SFD/BMD envelopes: compute Venv(x), Menv(x), then extract Vmax and Mmax.
  • Step C — Convert internal forces to stresses: use σ = My/I to estimate normal stress distribution; compute shear stress and glue-joint shear flow using q = VQ/I where appropriate.
  • Step D — Check multiple failure modes: tension yield, compression yield, local buckling (including “case 2” flange local buckling and “case 3” web local buckling), shear buckling, and glue-joint shear risk.
  • Step E — Factor of safety: compute FOS for each failure mode; the minimum FOS governs. Use the governing mode to drive the next iteration.
Two engineering insights that guided the final design:
  • Neutral axis target: since tension strength is ~5× compression strength, a symmetric HSS is suboptimal; raising ȳ reduces compression risk first.
  • Buckling dominance: thin plate buckling scales roughly with (t/b)2; long continuous compression panels have low buckling capacity unless subdivided or thickened.

4) Major iterations (design story)

Each iteration is a claim → justification → evidence → decision.

  • Iteration 1 — Square HSS (Excel): baseline cross-section. Learning: ȳ at mid-height is inefficient given tension/compression asymmetry; no diaphragms → high susceptibility to web buckling.
  • Iteration 2 — Add layers (Excel): raising ȳ helps but shows diminishing returns; many layers become material-inefficient.
  • Iteration 3 — Height sweep (MATLAB): increasing height improves compression-yield FOS but reduces web local buckling FOS; for 1000 N goal, height should be < 80 mm unless thickness increases heavily.
  • Iteration 4 — Width sweep (MATLAB): narrower width increases yield FOS while web buckling is largely unaffected; deck can overhang the structural box.
  • Iteration 5 — I-girder (Excel): attractive for yield efficiency, but high risk of case 2 local buckling, shear buckling, and glue-joint shear due to shear flow transfer demands.
  • Iteration 6 — Variable height (prototype + calculations): “match the BMD” concept; but behaves like a simply supported beam (little horizontal thrust), so forces remain bending-dominated; compression strength is much lower than tension strength, and long compression panels are buckling-governed with limited reserve capacity.
  • Iteration 7 — Discrete square frames: behaves like a ladder/open truss; weak continuous shear transfer → lower effective stiffness than a continuous box.
  • Iteration 8 — Triangular diaphragms (prototyping): upright orientation buckled easily; rotating the concept improved stability and informed the final infill orientation (triangles visible from the top).
  • Final — Truss box girder: redistribute plate area into triangular members below deck; concentrate material on upper/lower flanges; subdivide web panels to improve buckling capacity; convert much shear into axial forces in diagonals.
“Converge” moment: the final design was selected because it increased the minimum safety factor across failure modes while remaining buildable under time/material constraints.

5) CTMFs used + assessment (Frame · Diverge · Converge · Represent)

Not just naming tools: show evidence-of-use and evaluate utility/fit.

  • Frame — Requirements table + success metrics: I treated the project manual constraints as a “requirements spec” and wrote them as checkable statements (length, deck width, deck height multiple-of-20, unobstructed train passage, support flats).
    Utility: prevents late-stage “invalid build” failure; enables fast review before committing glue.
    Fit: extremely high for constrained physical builds.
  • Diverge — Parametric sweeps + rapid prototyping: Excel/MATLAB sweeps (height, width, layers) + paper prototypes for variable height and internal webs.
    Utility: cheap exploration; gives directional evidence before fabrication.
    Fit: high when failure mode is unclear early; reduces overconfidence in “intuitive” geometry.
  • Converge — Governing-mode elimination + risk-based selection: Concepts were rejected when dominated by buckling, glue shear, shear-path discontinuity, or constructibility risk (e.g., I-girder).
    Utility: keeps the team from optimizing the wrong metric.
    Fit: high in safety-critical-ish structures where “how it fails” matters more than “how strong it looks.”
  • Represent — Claim → justification → evidence writing: This page uses the pattern explicitly: each design decision is a claim, justified by mechanics reasoning and supported by iteration results / figures.
    Utility: makes grading fast and makes the design defensible.
    Fit: high whenever the audience includes assessors or future teammates.

7) Reflection

What this project changed in my design practice.

  • Key shift: I learned to treat failure modes as design constraints, not as after-the-fact explanations.
  • Practice upgrade: I now default to (1) requirements checklist, (2) fast parametric sweeps, (3) prototype a risky assumption, then (4) converge via governing-mode elimination.
  • Next iteration (if repeated): quantify glue-joint shear margins earlier; add an explicit decision matrix with criteria weights; and produce a compact evidence pack for each major design claim.
