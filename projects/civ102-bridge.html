1) Context & overview

Explain the project clearly to someone who has never taken CIV102.

This project is a first-year structural design challenge in CIV102（CIV102）. Our team designed and built a small-scale bridge using only mat-board（mat-board） and contact cement（contact cement）, then tested it under increasing load. The bridge had to span 1200 mm and satisfy geometric constraints (deck width/clearance, support flats), while maximizing performance and producing a defensible prediction of failure load and governing failure mode.

  • Objectives: span 1200 mm; minimum target load 400 N; maximize strength-to-weight; justify decisions using mechanics and evidence.
  • Constraints: restricted materials (mat-board + contact cement), strict geometry interface, and build quality/curing time as real performance variables.
  • Stakeholders: CIV102 teaching team (constraints + grading); Team 304 (performance + credibility); future readers (clarity + evidence trail).

3) Process interpretation & retroactive analysis

Re-frame CIV102 actions using Praxis language (Frame · Diverge · Converge · Represent).

Framing

We framed the bridge as a competition between failure modes rather than a single “strength” number. Early on, we treated the minimum factor of safety（FOS）across modes (compression-side yielding, local buckling, shear transfer/joint risk) as the decision driver. This framing prevented us from optimizing bending capacity in calculation while ignoring what could fail first in the real build: the joint.

Exploring (Diverge)

Our exploration combined parametric thinking (dimensions / reinforcement placement / internal support strategy) and targeted prototyping. We used the prototypes to test high-risk assumptions that models do not capture reliably: alignment tolerance, glue behavior, and whether slender plates buckle early under imperfections.

Converging

We converged on a box-girder-based concept reinforced with internal zigzag diaphragms, plus deck reinforcement aligned with bending demand. The goal was not only a higher predicted number, but reduced sensitivity to imperfections and higher reliability under realistic constraints.

Build & test results

  • Predicted: 1200 N (critical region: mid-span / mid-joint)
  • Tested peak load: 700 N
  • Observed failure: the middle glue joint opened because the adhesive had not fully cured, causing the bridge to split at the mid-span connection.
This result is not “math vs reality.” It is a reminder that process control (curing, joint discipline, build tolerance) is part of engineering design capacity.

5) CTMF assessments

Not just naming tools—explain how they were used, evaluate utility/fit, and state when to use/avoid.

  • CTMF 1 — Mathematical modeling（数学建模）
    How used: beam/section-property reasoning + failure-mode checks to predict capacity and identify the critical region (mid-span).
    Strengths: fast iteration; defensible tradeoffs; guides targeted reinforcement.
    Limitations: sensitive to assumptions (ideal bonds, perfect curing, simplified buckling).
    Use/avoid: use early to eliminate dominated concepts; avoid treating it as final truth without joint/process validation.
  • CTMF 2 — Prototyping & testing（原型与测试）
    How used: prototypes validated high-risk assumptions (alignment, diaphragm feasibility, joint reliability).
    Strengths: exposes real failure triggers (imperfections, glue behavior) models miss.
    Limitations: time-costly; noisy without a clear test plan.
    Use/avoid: prototype the riskiest assumption first; avoid full builds too early.
  • CTMF 3 — Decision matrix（决策矩阵）
    How used: retroactively, our selection compared predicted governing-mode FOS, weight penalty, fabrication risk, and credibility of prediction; “Decision evidence” figure is the compact artifact of this process.
    Strengths: makes criteria explicit; turns debate into traceable decisions.
    Limitations: arbitrary if criteria/weights are vague.
    Use/avoid: use when multiple plausible directions exist; define criteria first if you cannot measure.
  • CTMF 4 — Toulmin model（图尔敏模型）
    How used: structured our engineering argument as claim → evidence → warrant → rebuttal; the test outcome strengthened the rebuttal: process control can dominate capacity.
    Strengths: makes the argument legible; exposes missing evidence.
    Limitations: requires discipline—no evidence means gaps become obvious.
    Use/avoid: use for one-page summaries and prediction-vs-test explanations; avoid empty rhetoric without evidence.

9) Position reflection annotation

Connect this project to your design position: values, decisions, and what changed.

My design position is that engineering reasoning is only trustworthy when it stays inside a feedback loop with reality: evidence should not be an afterthought, but the mechanism that keeps models honest. CIV102 made this concrete. We predicted 1200 N and identified the mid-span/joint region as critical, but the bridge failed at 700 N because the adhesive had not fully cured. The location matched our concern, but the mechanism was a process variable—curing discipline—dominating real capacity.

This reshaped how I define “design.” A strong equation cannot compensate for a joint that is not ready; therefore scheduling, communication, and build discipline are part of the design system. This page is written to make that process legible: model → decision → build evidence → test outcome → updated beliefs.
