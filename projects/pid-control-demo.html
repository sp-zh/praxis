The three terms can be read as three different ways of reacting to error: P responds to the present, I accumulates the past, and D reacts to the trend of change. Together, they help reduce steady-state error, limit overshoot, and improve settling behavior.

What each term does

P — Proportional: pushes harder when the current error is larger. It usually makes the system faster, but too much can create oscillation and overshoot.

I — Integral: adds up past error. This helps remove steady-state error, but if the actuator saturates, the integral term can keep accumulating and create windup.

D — Derivative: reacts to the rate of error change. It can reduce overshoot and improve damping, but it is sensitive to measurement noise.

Common tuning patterns

  • Increase Kp for a quicker initial response.
  • Increase Ki to remove residual offset.
  • Increase Kd to damp oscillation and reduce overshoot.
  • Reduce gains if the response becomes unstable or too noisy.
  • Always check the effect of actuator saturation, not just the ideal response.

Engineering notes

  • Integral windup appears when the controller keeps integrating error even though the actuator is already saturated.
  • Anti-windup limits that accumulation so recovery after saturation is smoother.
  • Derivative filtering is often necessary because raw derivative action amplifies noise.
  • A good design is not the most complicated design. It is the simplest design that still meets requirements robustly.
