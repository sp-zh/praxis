1) One-Page Summary (Engineering Handbook style)

Audience: someone who knows nothing about this project

Pegasus Toronto is a day support program in the GTA for adults with developmental disabilities (“participants”). At the Matty Eckler Community Centre (MEC), participants create short films for the annual Pegasus Incredible Film Festival (PIFF). Jonathan enjoys filming with a GoPro HERO10 Black, but his limited arm strength and Cortical Visual Impairment (CVI) make it difficult to hold the camera at eye level and operate its low-contrast controls independently.

Community + opportunity framing

Instead of treating Pegasus as a “client” that tells us what to build, we approached the work as collaboration with community stakeholders (Jonathan, primary counsellors at MEC, and the film director Jerome). Our goal was to identify a verified, sustainable opportunity that improves lived experience: increasing Jonathan’s agency to record footage with greater control and independence, without compromising safety or recording quality.

Solution (what we are building toward)

WRC FILM is a wheelchair-mounted filming concept centered on a gimbal-based mechanism plus a monitor and simplified, high-contrast controls. The design aims to keep the viewing interface in Jonathan’s most usable field of vision, reduce physical strain, and enable controlled pan/tilt camera movement with minimal effort.

Why “Gimbal” is the recommended direction

After presenting three concepts (Handlebar / Gimbal / Slider) and performing an in-person verification session with Pegasus (Jonathan + Jerome + staff), the team explicitly recommended the Gimbal direction as the most feasible and lowest-strain option, while still preserving degrees of freedom for filming.

My position in this project (draft you can keep or edit)

In WRC FILM, I treated engineering as a way to expand agency for people who are routinely excluded by “default” product assumptions. Instead of optimizing for novelty or maximum features, I prioritized a design path that is learnable, low-strain, and resilient in real community contexts. This emphasis shaped how I interpreted “good engineering”: not just technical feasibility, but whether the system can be operated independently by the person it is meant to empower.

This project also forced me to confront a bias: at the start, I assumed a broader solution that benefits more participants is always better. Stakeholder engagement challenged that assumption—participants’ needs, goals, and capabilities vary dramatically, so “one-size-fits-all” can become a superficial form of inclusion. Focusing on Jonathan was not narrowing impact; it was choosing depth, dignity, and verified fit.

3) Concepts: Handlebar / Gimbal / Slider

We explored three concept families to reduce accessibility barriers for independent filming. The purpose of showing all three is to document the design space—not just the final answer.

Handlebar

A handle-driven interface intended to provide intuitive control through grasping and rotation/tilt gestures. Strength: strong “direct manipulation” feel. Risk: stability and safety if mounted overhead or outside the wheelchair footprint.

Caption: show the control idea + where instability risk comes from.

Gimbal Recommended

A gimbal-based mount + monitor that maintains stability while allowing pan/tilt control with minimal strain. This was judged the most feasible, low-strain direction after stakeholder verification.

Caption: highlight monitor distance + degrees of freedom + stability.

Slider

A table slider mechanism to translate the camera position. Risk: friction and sustained force requirements can cause visible strain; extended use may be tiring even if smoothed.

Chosen direction (explicit)

The concept recommended to move into the next phase is Gimbal. This page keeps the other concepts to document tradeoffs and evidence-based convergence.

Recommended Direction: WRC Gimbal (what it is + why it fits)

WRC FILM is the project umbrella; the gimbal is the core mechanism we recommend to carry forward. The design intent is to maximize independence while keeping the system safe, stable, and learnable within the real filming workflow at MEC.

What the system does

  • Keeps the viewing interface (monitor/visualizer) at a verified comfortable distance (e.g., ~35 cm observed in verification).
  • Provides pan/tilt degrees of freedom while minimizing vibration and physical strain.
  • Uses simplified, high-contrast, tactile controls to reduce reliance on low-contrast GoPro buttons.
  • Allows primary counsellors to set up quickly, then enables Jonathan to operate during filming.

Key constraints we designed around

Comfort: reduce sustained holding and awkward posture; prioritize learnability.
Quality: avoid visible camera shake; preserve usable footage.
Safety: avoid overhead failure modes; avoid blocking vision while driving.
Deployment: quick setup/disassembly; durable across repeated sessions.

4) CTMF Evaluations (Praxis II-specific, ≥3)

Each CTMF below is written as: (a) evidence of use, (b) utility & fit (critical evaluation), and (c) what I would change next time. Add screenshots/photos right inside each CTMF block to make “claim → evidence” obvious.

CTMF 1 — Request for Proposal (RFP): framing the opportunity

Evidence of use: We created a full RFP that defines the community context (Pegasus Toronto), primary stakeholder (Jonathan), stakeholder values, and a structured Need–Goals–Objectives (NGO) tree. This forced us to define measurable targets for comfort, safety, recording quality, deployment, and reliability.

Utility & fit: The RFP was extremely effective at preventing “solution-first” thinking. It made our assumptions explicit and gave us a shared definition of success. However, writing an RFP early can create a false sense of certainty: in our case, stakeholder verification later changed key numeric assumptions (e.g., monitor distance preference), which means the RFP must be treated as a living document rather than a final contract.

Next time: I would build an explicit “assumptions log” into the RFP and label which objectives are verified vs unverified to reduce overconfidence.

CTMF 2 — Functional Decomposition: turning “independent filming” into designable functions

Evidence of use: We decomposed the filming activity into smaller functions (e.g., stabilize camera, control pan/tilt, present a usable view, start/stop recording, mount safely, deploy quickly). This enabled modular ideation and mix-and-match concept building.

Utility & fit: Functional decomposition prevented us from locking onto a single physical form too early. It also made it easier to evaluate concepts fairly: a concept may “feel” elegant, but if it fails the “deploy safely” or “learn quickly” functions, it is not a good fit for the stakeholder context.

Limitations: Decomposition can hide interaction complexity. For accessibility work, “functions” are not independent—visual load, physical effort, and confidence/learnability interact. A purely modular view can underestimate cognitive and physical coupling.

Next time: I would pair decomposition with an explicit “interaction cost” layer (physical + visual + cognitive load).

CTMF 3 — Proxy Tests & Verification: learning faster than “full build”

Evidence of use: We brought physical proxies (cardboard handlebar sizing, a 3D-printed gimbal mockup, a working slider prototype) into an in-person verification meeting with Jonathan, Jerome, and Pegasus staff (March 30, 2026). This surfaced usability and strain signals that would not appear in CAD alone (e.g., slider strain; stability/safety concerns for handlebar; gimbal feasibility and ease-of-use feedback).

Utility & fit: Proxy testing was the single most decision-shaping step in convergence. It converted debates into measurable observations (distance preferences, degrees of freedom, stability). It also protected the stakeholder from being “tested on” by unfinished systems: prototypes were designed as questions, not as commitments.

Limitations: A single session risks overfitting to momentary conditions (fatigue, context, novelty effects). Proxy tests also need careful facilitation to avoid leading the stakeholder to say what we want to hear.

Next time: I would pre-register success criteria for each proxy test (what observation would make us stop / pivot / proceed), then repeat at least one key test across multiple sessions to confirm consistency.
