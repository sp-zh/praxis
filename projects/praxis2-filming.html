WRC FILM
Wheelchair-Mounted RC Filming Gimbal

An accessible filming concept that helps a community participant with CVI operate a camera more independently—without sacrificing stability, safety, or recording quality.

Community: Pegasus Toronto (Matty Eckler Community Centre) Stakeholder: Jonathan (CVI) Direction: Gimbal

1) Summary

Pegasus Toronto is a daytime support program in the Greater Toronto Area for adults with developmental disabilities (“Participants”). At the Matty Eckler Community Centre (MEC), Participants create short films for the annual Pegasus Film Festival (PIFF). Jonathan is our stakeholder, who enjoys shooting with a GoPro camera, but due to limited arm strength and cortical visual impairment (CVI), he struggles to hold the camera at eye level and operate the low-contrast controls independently. We designed a system to effectively help him complete independent filming.

Community + opportunity framing

Since Jonathan couldn't clearly articulate his ideas, we didn't treat Pegasus as a typical "client" to guide our development. Instead, we collaborated with stakeholders (Jonathan, MEC staff, and the film director Jerome(his father)). Our goal was to genuinely improve their life experience: enhancing their ability to independently record videos, giving them greater control and independence, without compromising safety or recording quality.

Solution

WRC FILM is a wheelchair filming system whose core components are a gimbal mechanism, a monitor, and a simplified, high-contrast control panel. The design aims to ensure the viewing interface is always within Jonathan's most convenient field of vision, reducing fatigue and enabling easy pan/tilt movements.

Recommendation

After comparing three concepts (Handlebar / Gimbal / Slider) and running proxy verification with stakeholders, we recommend advancing the Gimbal direction.

2) Annotation

In the WRC FILM project, I viewed engineering as a way to empower those excluded due to default product assumptions. They are often not in the mainstream spotlight, but they are still members of society and deserve attention. Instead of pursuing innovation or maximum functionality, we prioritized a learning-friendly, low-pressure workflow that adapts to real-world community shooting. This largely reflects one aspect of our team value: humanity.

3) Concepts

Handlebar

Strength: intuitive direct manipulation through grasping/rotation. Risk: Safety — The overhead mechanical structure spanning above the user’s head was confirmed to pose a safety hazard.

Gimbal Recommended

Gimbal + monitor + simplified controls. Strength: stability and degrees of freedom with low strain.

Slider

Translational camera movement via slider. Risk: friction / sustained force can cause strain over time.

4) Recommended direction

The concept recommended to move into the next phase is Gimbal.

5) CTMF evaluations

CTMF stands for Concepts, Tools, Models, and Frameworks. The goal is to build a curated record that supports future design work: each CTMF includes evidence of use, a critical evaluation of utility & fit, and what I would change next time. The set also covers the full design flow: Frame, Diverge, Converge, and Represent.

CTMF 1 — Request for Proposal (RFP) Frame

Evidence of use: We revised and refined the RFP to define community context, primary stakeholder constraints, and a structured Needs–Goals–Objectives framing that guided what we tested first.

Utility & fit: The RFP prevented “solution-first” thinking and aligned the team on what success means. The main risk is false certainty: early objectives can embed assumptions.

Next time: I would label which objectives are verified vs unverified and attach a short verification plan to each.

CTMF 2 — Functional Decomposition / Functional Basis Diverge

Evidence of use: We decomposed “independent filming” into designable functions (stabilize, control pan/tilt, provide usable view, start/stop recording, mount safely, deploy quickly). This enabled modular ideation across Handlebar / Gimbal / Slider.

Utility & fit: It helped us evaluate concepts fairly and expand the solution space while staying anchored to stakeholder needs. Its limitation is that function lists can hide hard integration couplings (stability vs adjustability, autonomy vs cognitive load).

Next time: I would explicitly map the toughest function interfaces and document which couplings are most likely to break.

🧠 Concepts 🧾 Evidence notes

CTMF 3 — Proxy Tests & Verification Converge

Evidence of use: We used proxy tests to evaluate “does this direction work in the real world?” before committing to high-fidelity builds. We tested interaction feasibility, stability, and usability signals, and used evidence to converge on the gimbal direction.

Utility & fit: Proxy testing converted debates into observable evidence and reduced the risk of building the wrong thing. The key tradeoff is ecological validity: tests must document conditions and be repeatable.

Next time: I would standardize a short test protocol (setup + conditions + pass/fail thresholds) and repeat the key test across sessions.

CTMF 4 — Visual Integration (Captions & Annotations) Represent

Evidence of use: We treated visuals as part of the engineering argument: concept photos, prototypes, and evidence are integrated so a reader can connect claims to supporting material quickly.

Utility & fit: Good visual integration makes the portfolio usable as a handbook. The risk is clutter; the best approach is one clear caption + a small number of high-signal annotations.

Next time: I would standardize captions into a consistent template so the page stays scannable while preserving engineering meaning.
