Annotation

Context → evidence → decision → outcome → reflection (portfolio style).

Many students take daily supplements (e.g., pills + protein). Yet the routine is surprisingly time-consuming: a timed baseline morning routine totalled 206 seconds, with 78 seconds spent just cleaning the cup. This is a strong signal that usability barriers are dominated by “maintenance friction,” not only dispensing speed.

We framed the opportunity as: reduce time, steps, and clutter while keeping dosing flexibility and reliability. We then used low-fidelity prototypes + proxy tests to make the decision evidence-based rather than preference-based.

My contribution: research on reference designs and constraints, reference design exploration to anchor feasibility, and prototype making to support proxy testing and comparison.

Stakeholders

Who benefits, who is affected, and what “success” means for them.

  • Primary users: Engineering Science students with rushed mornings and limited dorm space.
  • Secondary stakeholders: roommates impacted by clutter and shared cleaning areas.
  • Future constraints owners: manufacturers / retailers (cost, part count, robustness, ease of cleaning).

Requirements & criteria

From vague complaints → testable requirements.

  • Speed: aim for a ~40 s threshold for “simple tasks” as a usability target.
  • Refill frequency: at least 1 week between refills.
  • Powder dosage flexibility: support a wide usable range.
  • Steps: cap steps required to begin dispensing (effort minimization / usability).
  • Horizontal width: ≤ 40 cm to fit a dorm shelf footprint.
  • Pill dispensing: handle ~3 common pill types reliably.
  • Cleaning: target ≤ 40 seconds to clean all parts.

Concepts (Diverge)

We diverged across distinct mechanisms, not just minor variants.

  • Recommended: Rotating Cylinder Dispenser — 24 compartments (12 pills + 12 powders), two-piece rotating dropper, up to 11 days, 5 g measurement intervals.
  • Disk: 16 compartments with scoopers; strong storage but large footprint and cleaning complexity.
  • Pez Machine: lever + spring mechanism; qualitative reliability concerns and cleaning difficulty.
  • Slider with Slit: gravity-driven pill drop + powder hole; low steps but limited dosage control.
Note: the “shaker bottle” idea was explicitly removed during rescoping because it would be unfeasibly large to drink from.

Proxy tests

Low-fidelity prototypes, tested on the criteria that matter most.

  • Proxy tests measured dispensing speed, powder dosage accuracy (when applicable), step count, and cleaning time.
  • For most concepts, pills + powder were dispensed into a cup simultaneously to represent the fastest realistic routine.
  • The rotating cylinder prototype was 3D printed at small scale due to geometry difficulty in hand-making rotating parts.

Decision (Converge)

Measurement matrix + PUGH chart + pairwise comparison.

The decision used multiple lenses. In the measurement matrix, the rotating cylinder is consistently strong across key criteria and has a small footprint and fast cleaning. Pairwise comparison was especially useful when comparing rotating cylinder vs disk: both are similarly fast, but the disk has a much larger width and a more complex cleaning process (17 parts vs 2 parts).

Final concept

Rotating Cylinder Dispenser — what it is and why it works.

  • Architecture: two-piece rotating dropper system → low part count and easy cleaning.
  • Storage: 24 compartments (12 pills + 12 powders) → up to ~11 days before refilling.
  • Usability: 5 g measurement lines for adjustable dosing; user can cover pill dropper to separate pills from powders if desired.

CTMFs (4)

Each CTMF includes: tool → how used → evidence → assessment.

Frame

requirements + metrics

Tool: Requirements ranking + time-based success metrics.

  • How used: translated “annoying routine” into measurable criteria (speed, steps, cleaning, footprint, refill frequency).
  • Evidence: Appendix A baseline timing + Table 1 requirements.
  • Assessment: high utility; prevents feature creep and makes “tradeoffs” explicit early.

Diverge

mechanism-level options

Tool: Concept set + reference-driven ideation.

  • How used: explored distinct dispensing mechanisms (rotating, disk, Pez, slider) rather than minor tweaks.
  • Evidence: concept figures (Figures 1–4) + optional reference design screenshot.
  • Assessment: high fit; helped avoid early fixation and exposed constraints (size/cleaning/footprint).

Converge

evidence-based selection

Tool: Measurement matrix + PUGH chart (+ pairwise as tie-break).

  • How used: used proxy tests to populate comparable metrics, then converged with structured comparison.
  • Evidence: Table 2 + Table 3 (and Table 4 optional).
  • Assessment: strongest turning point; converts “preferences” into defensible rationale.

Represent

evidence trail

Tool: Claim → evidence → decision narrative, supported by annotated visuals.

  • How used: baseline friction → ranked requirements → concept set → test results → selection rationale → recommended concept.
  • Evidence: Appendix A + Table 1 + concept grid + Table 2/3 + embedded video.
  • Assessment: high utility for portfolio grading and future handoff; improves navigation and trust.

Reflection

What this changed in my design practice.

  • Key lesson: cleaning complexity (time + part count) is a dominant driver of real adoption, not a “secondary detail.”
  • Practice shift: I now default to requirements ranking + proxy tests early, then converge using explicit tradeoffs.
  • Next step: build a higher-fidelity prototype with real powder volumes and run longer-term “refill + cleaning” trials.
